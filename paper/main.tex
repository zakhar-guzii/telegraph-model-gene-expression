\begin{abstract}
Gene expression---the process by which a gene's DNA is read out to make messenger
RNA (mRNA), the molecule that carries its instructions---is a stochastic process:
even genetically identical cells in the same environment produce very different
numbers of mRNA molecules. The telegraph model explains the main source of this
variability. It treats a gene as a two-state (ON/OFF) switch that transcribes mRNA
only while active. We study the telegraph model in three independent ways and check
that all three agree: we (i) generate stochastic trajectories with the Gillespie
stochastic simulation algorithm (SSA); (ii) derive and solve a closed system of
ordinary differential equations (ODEs) for the first and second moments; and
(iii) draw independent samples directly from the analytic steady-state
distribution, using its Beta--Poisson--Bernoulli mixture form. The main
contribution is the cross-validation that links the three methods. The Law of Large
Numbers connects the SSA to the moment ODEs in finite time; the long-time limit of
the ODEs connects them to the steady-state sampler; and a long SSA run closes the
triangle. The code is available at
\url{https://github.com/zakhar-guzii/telegraph-model-gene-expression}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

A surprising fact of molecular biology is that gene expression is variable. Two
cells with identical DNA, grown side by side in the same medium, contain different
numbers of copies of the same mRNA or protein. This cell-to-cell variability, known
as phenotypic noise, is an inherent consequence of molecular interactions rather
than a measurement artifact. The molecules involved---one copy of a gene, a few
transcription factors (regulatory proteins that bind DNA and control gene activity),
a few dozen mRNA molecules---are present in such small numbers that the discreteness
and randomness of individual chemical events cannot be averaged away. The promoter
(the DNA region that controls when a gene is switched on) switches between an active
and an inactive state at random times. While active, it produces mRNA molecules
through transcription (the synthesis of mRNA from the gene's DNA) in random bursts.
Each mRNA molecule is then degraded---broken down and removed---at a random time. As
a result, the mRNA count is a discrete-valued stochastic process, not a
deterministic quantity.

The telegraph model is the simplest framework that reproduces this behaviour. It
idealises the gene as a two-state device---like a telegraph key that is either
pressed (ON) or released (OFF)---that produces mRNA only while ON. Despite its
simplicity, it reproduces the bursty, over-dispersed (variance larger than the
Poisson baseline) mRNA distributions seen in single-molecule experiments, which
count individual mRNAs inside single cells~\cite{raj2006}, and it can be analysed
exactly~\cite{peccoud1995}.

We treat the telegraph model as a case study in how a stochastic process can be
described from several mathematically distinct viewpoints. We implement three
approaches that look unrelated:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item a stochastic simulation (the SSA), which answers ``what does a single cell
    actually do?'';
  \item a system of moment ODEs, which answers ``what does the average cell do, and
    how spread out is the population?'';
  \item a steady-state sampler, which answers ``what does the population look like
    after a very long time?''.
\end{enumerate}

Each approach is correct by itself. But only by comparing them can we be sure that
all three are built correctly and that our understanding of the model is right.

\section{Model Formulation}
\label{sec:model}

The state of the system at time $t$ is the pair
\begin{equation}
  \big(G(t),\,R(t)\big), \qquad G(t)\in\{0,1\}, \quad R(t)\in\mathbb{N},
\end{equation}
where the binary variable $G$ records whether the gene is OFF ($G=0$) or ON
($G=1$), and the integer $R$ counts the number of mRNA molecules present. The dynamics are specified by four elementary reactions, each with a
propensity $a_j$ giving the immediate rate at which it fires:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{llll}
\toprule
\textbf{Reaction} & \textbf{Description} & \textbf{Effect} & \textbf{Propensity} \\
\midrule
$R_1$ & Gene activation   & $G:0\to1$       & $a_1 = \kon\,(1-G)$ \\
$R_2$ & Gene inactivation & $G:1\to0$       & $a_2 = \koff\,G$ \\
$R_3$ & RNA synthesis     & $R\to R+1$      & $a_3 = \ksyn\,G$ \\
$R_4$ & RNA degradation   & $R\to R-1$      & $a_4 = \kdeg\,R$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The four rate constants are all positive. The switching rates $\kon$ and $\koff$
control how quickly the promoter turns on and off; their ratio sets the long-run fraction of time the gene is active. The synthesis rate $\ksyn$ is the rate at
which mRNA is produced while the gene is ON; the factor $G$ sets transcription to zero when $G=0$. The degradation rate $\kdeg$ is the per-molecule decay rate, so the total degradation propensity $\kdeg R$ is proportional to the amount of mRNA present. Together, the rates $(\kon,\koff,\ksyn,\kdeg)$ and an initial condition
$(g_0,r_0)$ at time $t_0$ fully determine the law of the process.

The waiting time before each reaction is exponentially distributed and depends only
on the current state, so $(G(t),R(t))$ is a continuous-time Markov chain (CTMC): a
process that jumps between discrete states, where the next state depends only on the
present one. Its probability distribution $P_t(g,r)=\Pr\{G(t)=g,\,R(t)=r\}$ evolves
according to the chemical master equation~\cite{wilkinson,ingalls}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:master}
\begin{aligned}
  \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t} P_t(g,r)
  ={}& \kon\big[\delta_{g,1}P_t(0,r) - \delta_{g,0}P_t(g,r)\big] \\
   &+ \koff\big[\delta_{g,0}P_t(1,r) - \delta_{g,1}P_t(g,r)\big] \\
   &+ \ksyn\,\delta_{g,1}\big[P_t(1,r-1) - P_t(1,r)\big] \\
   &+ \kdeg\big[(r+1)P_t(g,r+1) - r\,P_t(g,r)\big],
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
with the convention $P_t(g,-1)=0$. Equation~\eqref{eq:master} underlies all three approaches below: the SSA samples trajectories whose distribution solves it, the moment ODEs are derived from it exactly, and the steady-state sampler draws from its $t\to\infty$ fixed point.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

 The SSA produces individual random trajectories; averaging many of them estimates the moments. The moment ODEs compute those same moments directly and without sampling noise. The steady-state sampler produces the distribution the first two settle into as $t\to\infty$.  

\subsection{Stochastic Simulation (Gillespie SSA)}
\label{sec:ssa}

The most direct way to study a stochastic process is to simulate sample paths of
it. The \emph{stochastic simulation algorithm} (SSA), introduced by
Gillespie~\cite{gillespie1976,gillespie1977} and reviewed
in~\cite{gillespie2007}, generates statistically exact sample paths of a CTMC. It
is exact in the sense that it makes no approximation to the master
equation~\eqref{eq:master}. The key idea is that, given the current state, two
questions can be answered separately: when does the next reaction occur, and which
reaction is it?

Let $M=4$ be the number of reactions and let
$a_0 = \sum_{j=1}^{M} a_j$ be the \emph{total propensity}, i.e.\ the combined rate
at which any reaction happens in the current state. Then:
\begin{itemize}
  \item the waiting time $\tau$ until the next event is exponentially
    distributed, $\tau \sim \mathrm{Exp}(a_0)$, with mean $1/a_0$. Intuitively,
    the larger $a_0$, the sooner the next event occurs.
  \item the reaction that fires is reaction $j$ with probability proportional to
    its propensity,
    \begin{equation}
      \Pr(j) = \frac{a_j}{a_0}.
    \end{equation}
    In words: a faster reaction is more likely to be the one that happens next,
    in exact proportion to its share of the total rate.
\end{itemize}

In practice each step draws two independent uniform random numbers
$r_1,r_2\sim\mathrm{U}(0,1)$ on $[0,1]$ and sets
\begin{equation}
  \tau = \frac{1}{a_0}\,\ln\!\frac{1}{r_1},
  \qquad
  j = \min\Big\{ k : \textstyle\sum_{i=1}^{k} a_i > r_2\,a_0 \Big\}.
\end{equation}
These two expressions apply the rules above by inversion sampling. The first turns a uniform draw into an exponential waiting time. The second selects a reaction by summing the propensities in order until the partial sum first exceeds the fraction $r_2 a_0$ of the total, so reaction $j$ is chosen with probability $a_j/a_0$. The time is then advanced by $\tau$, the state is updated by reaction $j$, the propensities are recomputed, and the loop repeats for a fixed number of events $n_{\mathrm{sim}}$. If the total propensity reaches $a_0=0$, no reaction can fire, so the state is absorbing and is held fixed afterwards.

A single trajectory is the history of one cell. To obtain population statistics, we run $N_{\mathrm{rep}}$ independent trajectories with identical parameters and
average over them. Each trajectory advances by its own random time increments, so the same event index occurs at different times in different trajectories. We therefore resample every trajectory onto a shared uniform time grid by a zero-order hold before averaging; the state is piecewise constant between events, so this introduces no error. The empirical moments are then ensemble averages at each grid time, for example
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ssa-mean}
  \widehat{\muR}(t)
  = \frac{1}{N_{\mathrm{rep}}}\sum_{i=1}^{N_{\mathrm{rep}}} R_i(t),
  \qquad
  \widehat{\muG}(t)
  = \frac{1}{N_{\mathrm{rep}}}\sum_{i=1}^{N_{\mathrm{rep}}} G_i(t),
\end{equation}
with sample variances and the sample gene--RNA covariance defined analogously.

\subsection{Moment ODEs}
\label{sec:ode}

The SSA describes individual cells. Often we instead want deterministic equations for population-level statistics---the \emph{moments}. A moment is an expectation: the mean $\muR=\E[R]$ is the average mRNA level, the variance
$\sigRsq=\E[R^2]-\E[R]^2$ measures the spread (the noise), and the covariance
$\CRG=\E[RG]-\E[R]\E[G]$ measures how gene activity and mRNA level vary together.

Let $f(G,R)$ be any \emph{observable}, that is, any function of the state; for
example, $f=R$ gives the mean and $f=R^2$ gives the second moment. Multiplying the master equation~\eqref{eq:master} by $f$ and summing over all states gives the general moment evolution rule
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:moment-rule}
  \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t}\,\E\big[f(G,R)\big]
  = \E\!\left[\sum_{j=1}^{M} a_j(G,R)\,\big(f(G+\Delta G_j,\,R+\Delta R_j) - f(G,R)\big)\right],
\end{equation}
where $(\Delta G_j,\Delta R_j)$ is the change that reaction $j$ makes to the state
$(G,R)$, and $\E[\cdot]$ is the expectation over the process. So, the
expected rate of change of $f$ is the sum over reactions of the firing rate times
the change in $f$. We apply this rule term by term, choosing $f$ to extract each
moment.

\paragraph{Means.}
Taking $f=G$, only the switching reactions $R_1$ ($\Delta G=+1$) and $R_2$
($\Delta G=-1$) change the gene state and so contribute, giving
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dmuG}
  \frac{\mathrm{d}\muG}{\mathrm{d}t}
  = \kon\,(1-\muG) - \koff\,\muG .
\end{equation}
Here activation raises the mean activity toward $1$ at rate $\kon$, acting on the
inactive fraction $1-\muG$, and inactivation lowers it toward $0$ at rate $\koff$,
acting on the active fraction $\muG$. Taking $f=R$, only synthesis ($\Delta R=+1$,
propensity $\ksyn G$) and degradation ($\Delta R=-1$, propensity $\kdeg R$)
contribute:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dmuR}
  \frac{\mathrm{d}\muR}{\mathrm{d}t}
  = \ksyn\,\muG - \kdeg\,\muR .
\end{equation}
That is, mRNA is produced in proportion to the active fraction $\muG$ and removed in proportion to the amount present, $\muR$.

\paragraph{Variance of mRNA.}
For $f=R^2$, synthesis contributes $(R+1)^2-R^2 = 2R+1$ and degradation contributes $(R-1)^2-R^2 = -2R+1$, so by~\eqref{eq:moment-rule},
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\mathrm{d}\,\E[R^2]}{\mathrm{d}t}
  = 2\ksyn\,\E[GR] + \ksyn\,\muG - 2\kdeg\,\E[R^2] + \kdeg\,\muR .
\end{equation}
Differentiating the definition $\sigRsq=\E[R^2]-\muR^2$ and substituting
\eqref{eq:dmuR} for $\mathrm{d}\muR/\mathrm{d}t$, the $\muR^2$ terms collect into the covariance and variance, giving
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dsigR}
  \frac{\mathrm{d}\sigRsq}{\mathrm{d}t}
  = 2\ksyn\,\CRG - 2\kdeg\,\sigRsq + \ksyn\,\muG + \kdeg\,\muR .
\end{equation}
The mRNA variance is driven up by coupling to gene activity (the $\CRG$ term) and by the randomness of production and decay, and driven down by degradation (the $-2\kdeg$ term).

\paragraph{Gene--RNA covariance.}
For $f=GR$, the four reactions change $GR$ by $R$ (activation), $-R$
(inactivation), $G$ (synthesis), and $-G$ (degradation), each weighted by its
propensity. Using $G^2=G$ (because $G\in\{0,1\}$), one obtains
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\mathrm{d}\,\E[GR]}{\mathrm{d}t}
  = \kon\,\muR + \ksyn\,\muG - (\kon+\koff+\kdeg)\,\E[GR].
\end{equation}
Differentiating the definition $\CRG = \E[GR]-\muG\muR$ and substituting the mean
equations~\eqref{eq:dmuG}--\eqref{eq:dmuR}, the linear terms combine into
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dCRG}
  \frac{\mathrm{d}\CRG}{\mathrm{d}t}
  = \ksyn\,\sigGsq - (\kon+\koff+\kdeg)\,\CRG .
\end{equation}
The gene--mRNA covariance is generated by transcription, which transfers the gene's
fluctuations $\sigGsq$ to the mRNA (the $\ksyn\sigGsq$ term), and reduced at the
combined rate of switching and degradation.

\paragraph{Exact closure.}
In a general nonlinear reaction system, the equations for low moments depend on higher moments, so the hierarchy never closes and one must use approximate moment-closure schemes~\cite{ingalls,wilkinson}. Here the system closes \emph{exactly}. The only nonlinearity would come from the variance of the gene state, but because $G$ is binary,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:closure}
  \sigGsq = \E[G^2]-\muG^2 = \E[G]-\muG^2 = \muG\,(1-\muG),
\end{equation}
which depends on the mean $\muG$ alone. Substituting~\eqref{eq:closure}
into~\eqref{eq:dCRG} makes the four equations
\eqref{eq:dmuG},~\eqref{eq:dmuR},~\eqref{eq:dsigR},~\eqref{eq:dCRG} a
\emph{closed, exact} linear system in $(\muG,\muR,\sigRsq,\CRG)$. This is not an
approximation: the solutions are the true moments of the master
equation~\eqref{eq:master}. We integrate them numerically from the initial
conditions $\muG(t_0)=g_0$, $\muR(t_0)=r_0$, $\sigRsq(t_0)=\CRG(t_0)=0$ using an
explicit Runge--Kutta (RK45) scheme.

\subsection{Steady-State Sampler}
\label{sec:ss}

As $t\to\infty$ the telegraph model forgets its initial condition and converges to
a unique stationary distribution $P_\infty(g,r)$. Instead of simulating a long
transient to reach it, we can sample from $P_\infty$ directly, because it has a representation as a \emph{Beta--Poisson--Bernoulli
mixture}~\cite{peccoud1995,raj2006}.

The hierarchy consists of three conditional steps. First, the long-run probability
that the gene is active varies from cell to cell; this \emph{mixing fraction}
$p\in[0,1]$ follows a Beta distribution whose shape is set by the switching rates
(measured in units of the degradation rate $\kdeg$, so $\kdeg$ fixes the unit of
time). Second, conditional on $p$, the gene state is Bernoulli with probability $p$
of being ON. Third, conditional on the gene being active, the mRNA count is Poisson
with mean $\lambda$ equal to the \emph{burst size} $\ksyn/\kdeg$ (the mRNAs made per
mRNA lifetime while ON) scaled by $p$. Introducing the dimensionless switching
rates $a=\kon/\kdeg$ and $b=\koff/\kdeg$, each independent sample is drawn as:
\begin{align}
  p &\sim \mathrm{Beta}(a,b),
  &&f(p) = \frac{\Gamma(a+b)}{\Gamma(a)\,\Gamma(b)}\,p^{\,a-1}(1-p)^{\,b-1}, \\[2pt]
  G \mid p &\sim \mathrm{Bernoulli}(p),
  &&\Pr(G=1\mid p)=p,\quad \Pr(G=0\mid p)=1-p, \\[2pt]
  R \mid G,p &\sim \mathrm{Poisson}(\lambda),
  &&\lambda = \frac{\ksyn}{\kdeg}\,p,
\end{align}
with $R=0$ whenever $G=0$ (a silent gene makes no mRNA). The joint stationary law
is obtained by averaging these conditional distributions over the mixing fraction:
\begin{equation}
  P_\infty(g,r) = \int_0^1 \Pr(G=g\mid p)\,\Pr(R=r\mid g,p)\,f(p)\,\mathrm{d}p .
\end{equation}
The integral marginalises over $p$: it sums the probability of each $(g,r)$ outcome
over all values of $p$, weighted by the density $f(p)$. Drawing $N_{\mathrm{rep}}$
triples $(p,G,R)$ and averaging gives unbiased Monte Carlo estimates of the
steady-state moments $\widehat{\muG}^{\,ss},\widehat{\muR}^{\,ss}$, and so on. Two
edge cases are handled explicitly. If $\koff=0$, the gene is constitutively active,
so $p\equiv1$ and $R\sim\mathrm{Poisson}(\ksyn/\kdeg)$. If $\kdeg=0$, no steady
state exists (mRNA accumulates without bound), and the input is rejected. The exact
stationary means used as the reference below are
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ss-means}
  \muG^{ss} = \frac{\kon}{\kon+\koff},
  \qquad
  \muR^{ss} = \frac{\ksyn}{\kdeg}\,\muG^{ss},
\end{equation}
obtained by setting the time derivatives in~\eqref{eq:dmuG}--\eqref{eq:dmuR} to
zero.

\subsection{The Validation Triangle}
\label{sec:intersection}

The three methods are different mathematical objects, but probability theory
predicts exact relationships between them. We state these as the three edges of a
\emph{validation triangle} and test each one numerically in the experiments that
follow.

\paragraph{Edge 1 --- SSA $\leftrightarrow$ Moment ODEs (finite time).}
The empirical averages~\eqref{eq:ssa-mean} are sample means of independent,
identically distributed trajectories. By the Law of Large
Numbers~\cite{vankampen}, as $N_{\mathrm{rep}}\to\infty$ they converge to the true
expectations, which are exactly the solutions of the moment ODEs:
\begin{equation}
  \lim_{N_{\mathrm{rep}}\to\infty}
  \frac{1}{N_{\mathrm{rep}}}\sum_{i=1}^{N_{\mathrm{rep}}} G_i(t) = \muG(t),
  \qquad
  \lim_{N_{\mathrm{rep}}\to\infty}
  \frac{1}{N_{\mathrm{rep}}}\sum_{i=1}^{N_{\mathrm{rep}}} R_i(t) = \muR(t),
\end{equation}
and likewise the empirical covariance converges to $\CRG(t)$. This links the
stochastic and deterministic descriptions: with finitely many replicates, the SSA should follow the ODE curves up to a statistical error of order
$1/\sqrt{N_{\mathrm{rep}}}$.

\paragraph{Edge 2 --- Moment ODEs $\leftrightarrow$ Steady-state sampler (long time).}
The stationary distribution is the $t\to\infty$ limit of the dynamics, so the
moment ODEs must approach a fixed point,
\begin{equation}
  \lim_{t\to\infty}\muG(t) = \muG^{ss},
  \qquad
  \lim_{t\to\infty}\muR(t) = \muR^{ss},
  \qquad \text{etc.,}
\end{equation}
which coincides with~\eqref{eq:ss-means}. The independent steady-state sampler must
estimate \emph{these same} values,
\begin{equation}
  \lim_{N_{\mathrm{rep}}\to\infty}
  \frac{1}{N_{\mathrm{rep}}}\sum_{i=1}^{N_{\mathrm{rep}}} G_i^{ss} = \muG^{ss},
  \qquad
  \lim_{N_{\mathrm{rep}}\to\infty}
  \frac{1}{N_{\mathrm{rep}}}\sum_{i=1}^{N_{\mathrm{rep}}} R_i^{ss} = \muR^{ss}.
\end{equation}
By the Central Limit Theorem~\cite{gardiner}, the sampler's error decays as
$\sigma/\sqrt{N_{\mathrm{rep}}}$, where $\sigma$ is the standard deviation of the
stationary distribution. Observing this $1/\sqrt{N_{\mathrm{rep}}}$ rate confirms
that the sampler is a valid Monte Carlo estimator of the steady-state moments.

\paragraph{Edge 3 --- SSA $\leftrightarrow$ Steady-state sampler (closing the triangle).}
An SSA trajectory run long enough to reach stationarity samples the same
distribution $P_\infty$ as the direct sampler. Hence the long-time plateau of the
SSA moments must agree with both the ODE fixed point and the sampler
estimates.

\subsection{Parameter Exploration (Notebook 1)}
\label{sec:res-explore}

This notebook runs the SSA alone and checks that it reproduces the qualitative
behaviour expected when the gene's parameters change, varying one effect at a time
so that each plot isolates a single mechanism.

The first parameter is the \emph{speed of promoter switching}. Biologically, this
is the difference between a gene that switches ON and OFF rarely---staying in each state longer than an mRNA molecule survives---and one that switches many times within a single mRNA lifetime. The regime is set by how $\kon$ and $\koff$ compare with the degradation rate $\kdeg$. This ratio changes the variance $\sigRsq$ and
the gene--RNA covariance $\CRG$ while leaving the mean $\muR$ fixed.
Figure~\ref{fig:wf-switch} confirms this: slow switching ($\kon=\koff=0.05$)
produces long transcriptional bursts (short episodes in which an active gene makes
many mRNAs), a wide $\pm\sigma_R$ band, and a strong covariance, whereas fast
switching ($\kon=\koff=10$) averages the promoter into a quasi-constitutive state
(effectively always on) with a narrow band and near-zero covariance. Both reach the
same mean $\muR\approx10$.

  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.49\textwidth]{param_switching_slow.png}
  \hfill
  \includegraphics[width=0.49\textwidth]{param_switching_fast.png}
  \caption{Switching speed reshapes noise at fixed mean. Slow switching (left,
    $\kon=\koff=0.05$) gives long bursts, a wide $\pm\sigma_R$ band and strong
    gene--RNA covariance; fast switching (right, $\kon=\koff=10$) flickers so
    quickly that the promoter averages out, leaving a narrow band and near-zero
    covariance. Both reach the same mean $\muR\approx10$.}
  \label{fig:wf-switch}
\end{figure}

The second parameter is the \emph{level} of expression. In real cells this depends
mainly on promoter architecture---the arrangement of DNA sequence elements that
sets how strongly a promoter fires. A strong promoter carrying a TATA box and an
initiator (Inr) element (two common core-promoter sequences) sustains the ON state
and produces much mRNA, while a TATA-less promoter falls silent quickly. In the
model, with $\kon$ fixed, the steady-state mean is governed by the burst size
$\ksyn/\koff$; this experiment changes how much mRNA each burst makes, not how often
bursts occur. Figure~\ref{fig:wf-expr} shows this: the high-expression promoter
($\koff=4,\ksyn=56$, burst $\approx14$) reaches a large $\muR$, while the
low-expression one ($\koff=10,\ksyn=30$, burst $\approx3$) produces few molecules
with large relative noise.

The third experiment tests whether these steady states are properties of the
dynamics or artefacts of the initial state. We run the \emph{same} rates from two
very different initial states: a resting cell in the G1 phase (the growth phase of
the cell cycle before DNA is copied) with no mRNA, and a cell at the peak of a burst
with the gene ON and fifteen transcripts present. Both must converge to the same
fixed point, and Figure~\ref{fig:wf-init} shows this: the inactive start rises from
zero while the active-burst start decays from above, and the two curves meet at the
same steady state. The process forgets its initial condition, as expected for an
ergodic Markov chain (one that reaches a unique long-run distribution regardless of
where it starts).

The fourth experiment examines the \emph{numerical} parameters. These do not change
the biology, only the quality of the estimate: the sampling error scales as
$1/\sqrt{N_{\mathrm{rep}}}$, with additional bias if runs are too short to reach
stationarity. Here $N_{\mathrm{rep}}=224$ matches the 224 sequenced fibroblasts
(a common connective-tissue cell type) of a reference single-cell dataset.
Figure~\ref{fig:wf-numeric} contrasts an under-resolved run
($n_{\mathrm{sim}}=1000$), whose moments are jagged, with the experiment-scale run
($N_{\mathrm{rep}}=224,\ n_{\mathrm{sim}}=5000$), which reaches steady state and
gives smooth estimates.

\subsection{SSA vs.\ ODE: Moment Dynamics (Edge 1, Notebook 2)}
\label{sec:res-ssa-ode}

\emph{Aim.} Verify that the stochastic simulation and the deterministic moment ODEs
describe the same dynamics at all times (Edge~1 of the validation triangle,
Section~\ref{sec:intersection}). If they agree, the SSA ensemble averages should
track the ODE curves up to sampling noise of order $1/\sqrt{N_{\mathrm{rep}}}$.

Figure~\ref{fig:ssa_moments} shows the moment dynamics from the Gillespie SSA.
Starting from a silent gene with no mRNA, the mean activity $\muG(t)$ relaxes to
its stationary value $0.5$, the mean mRNA count $\muR(t)$ rises toward $10$, and the
gene--RNA covariance $\CRG(t)$ rises and then plateaus. The shaded $\pm\sigma$ bands
show the noise: the spread in mRNA count across cells is large (of order the mean),
which is the phenotypic variability the telegraph model is designed to capture.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{ssa_moments.png}
  \caption{Time evolution of ensemble-averaged moments from the Gillespie SSA.
    (A) Mean gene activity $\mu_G(t)$ with $\pm\sigma_G$ fluctuation bands.
    (B) Mean mRNA count $\mu_R(t)$ with $\pm\sigma_R$ fluctuation bands.
    (C) Gene--RNA covariance $C_{RG}(t)$. Parameters: $k_{\mathrm{on}}=0.5$,
    $k_{\mathrm{off}}=0.5$, $k_{\mathrm{syn}}=20.0$, $k_{\mathrm{deg}}=1.0$,
    $N_{\mathrm{rep}}=1000$.}
  \label{fig:ssa_moments}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:ode_moments} shows the corresponding deterministic dynamics from
the closed ODE system~\eqref{eq:dmuG}--\eqref{eq:dCRG}. The curves are smooth, since
they are exact expectations rather than estimates, but their shapes, timescales,
and limiting values match the stochastic results panel for panel.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{ode_moments.png}
  \caption{Time evolution of the deterministic moment ODEs corresponding to the
    same parameters as in Figure~\ref{fig:ssa_moments}. (A) Mean gene activity
    $\mu_G(t)$. (B) Mean mRNA count $\mu_R(t)$. (C) Gene--RNA covariance
    $C_{RG}(t)$.}
  \label{fig:ode_moments}
\end{figure}

\emph{Result.} The direct test is the overlay in Figure~\ref{fig:ssa_vs_ode}. The
SSA ensemble averages (green, with $\pm\sigma$ bands) lie on top of the
deterministic ODE curves (dashed blue) in all three panels, throughout the
transient and into the steady state. The only visible difference is a small wiggle
in the SSA covariance curve; this is the expected finite-$N_{\mathrm{rep}}$ sampling
fluctuation and shrinks as more trajectories are added. The agreement is not limited
to this symmetric case: Notebook~2 repeats the comparison across slow and fast
switching, high and low expression, and both initial conditions, and the SSA means
fall on the ODE curves in every regime. Figure~\ref{fig:wf-ssaode} shows the hardest
case, slow switching, where the noise is largest; the SSA still tracks the ODE
throughout.

  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{ssa_vs_ode.png}
  \caption{Comparison of the SSA ensemble averages (with $\pm\sigma$ fluctuation
    bands) and the deterministic ODE solutions. The SSA results closely match the

  \label{fig:ssa_vs_ode}

\emph{Conclusion.} The SSA converges to the exact moment dynamics, as predicted by
the Law of Large Numbers, so Edge~1 holds for any parameters. The $\pm\sigma$ bands
do \emph{not} collapse onto the mean: they measure genuine cell-to-cell variability,
which persists no matter how many trajectories are averaged and is correctly
predicted by the variance equation~\eqref{eq:dsigR}.

\subsection{Steady-State Validation (Edges 2 \& 3, Notebook 3)}
\label{sec:res-ss}

\emph{Aim.} Verify that the long-time limit of the moment ODEs agrees with the
independent steady-state sampler (Edge~2), that the sampler's error decays at the
Central Limit Theorem rate $1/\sqrt{N_{\mathrm{rep}}}$, and that both reduce
correctly to known limits at the boundaries of parameter space (Edge cases).

\emph{Result.} The ODE solutions in Figures~\ref{fig:ode_moments}
and~\ref{fig:ssa_vs_ode} plateau at $\muG^{ss}=0.5$ and $\muR^{ss}=10$, matching the
analytical fixed point~\eqref{eq:ss-means}. The direct sampler reproduces these
values: with $N_{\mathrm{rep}}=2\times10^5$ samples it returns
$\widehat{\muG}^{\,ss}\approx0.499$ and $\widehat{\muR}^{\,ss}\approx10.02$, agreeing
to within Monte Carlo error. Figure~\ref{fig:ss_convergence} makes the convergence
quantitative: on log--log axes, the mean absolute deviation of the sampler's
$\muR^{ss}$ estimate from the exact value follows a line of slope $-\tfrac12$,
matching the $1/\sqrt{N_{\mathrm{rep}}}$ reference over four decades of sample size.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{steady_state_convergence.png}
  \caption{Convergence of the exact steady-state sampler's empirical moments to
    the analytical steady-state values as a function of the number of samples
    $N_{\mathrm{rep}}$. The error (shown as mean absolute deviation) decays as
    $1/\sqrt{N_{\mathrm{rep}}}$, as expected from the Central Limit Theorem.}
  \label{fig:ss_convergence}
\end{figure}

A more demanding check asks the sampler to reproduce \emph{all five} steady-state
moments at once. For $\kon=0.5,\koff=1,\ksyn=20,\kdeg=1$ (so $\muR^{ss}=6.667$), each
moment has a closed form,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ss-closed}
  \muG^{ss}=\frac{\kon}{\kon+\koff},\quad
  \muR^{ss}=\frac{\ksyn}{\kdeg}\muG^{ss},\quad
  \CRG^{ss}=\frac{\ksyn\,\muG^{ss}(1-\muG^{ss})}{\kon+\koff+\kdeg},
\end{equation}
together with $\sigma_G^{ss}=\sqrt{\muG^{ss}(1-\muG^{ss})}$ and
$\sigma_R^{ss}=\sqrt{\muR^{ss}+(\ksyn/\kdeg)\,\CRG^{ss}}$. At
$N_{\mathrm{rep}}=2\times10^5$, the sampler matches every moment to within $0.04$
(Table~\ref{tab:moments}), and the absolute error in $\muR$ shows the same
$1/\sqrt{N_{\mathrm{rep}}}$ decay, falling from $3.87$ at $N_{\mathrm{rep}}=10$ to
$0.0145$ at $N_{\mathrm{rep}}=10^5$.

  \centering

  \textbf{Moment} & \textbf{Analytic} & \textbf{Sampler} & \textbf{Abs.\ diff.} \\
  \midrule
  $\muG^{ss}$    & 0.3333 & 0.3317 & 0.0016 \\
  $\muR^{ss}$    & 6.6667 & 6.6277 & 0.0389 \\
  $\sigma_G^{ss}$ & 0.4714 & 0.4708 & 0.0006 \\
  $\sigma_R^{ss}$ & 6.4979 & 6.4767 & 0.0212 \\
  $\CRG^{ss}$    & 1.7778 & 1.7666 & 0.0111 \\
  \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
  \caption{Steady-state moments from the direct sampler ($N_{\mathrm{rep}}=2\times10^5$)
    against their closed-form values~\eqref{eq:ss-closed}, for
    $\kon=0.5,\koff=1,\ksyn=20,\kdeg=1$.}
  \label{tab:moments}
\end{table}

Finally, we test two limits where the model reduces to a known result. When the gene
never switches off ($\koff=0$), it is constitutively active and the telegraph model
reduces to a birth--death process---a Markov chain whose only moves are $+1$ and
$-1$---whose stationary law is a Poisson distribution; the moment ODEs accordingly
relax to $\muG^{ss}=1$, $\muR^{ss}=\ksyn/\kdeg$. Figure~\ref{fig:wf-poisson} overlays
the sampled counts on the analytic Poisson PMF; they coincide, with $\muR\approx9.99$
and a Fano factor (the variance-to-mean ratio) $\approx0.99$, against the expected
mean $\ksyn/\kdeg=10$ and Fano factor $1$. In the opposite limit, with no degradation
($\kdeg=0$), mRNA accumulates without bound and no steady state exists; the sampler
detects this and raises a \texttt{ValueError} instead of returning an invalid result.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{validation_poisson.png}
  \caption{Edge case $\koff=0$ (constitutively active gene). The sampled
    steady-state mRNA distribution matches the analytic Poisson PMF with mean
    $\ksyn/\kdeg=10$ (Fano factor $\approx1$), the exact limit the telegraph model
    must reduce to when the gene never switches off.}
  \label{fig:wf-poisson}
\end{figure}

\emph{Conclusion.} The sampler is an unbiased, $\sqrt{N}$-consistent estimator of the
steady-state moments, so Edge~2 holds. Because the SSA moments
(Figure~\ref{fig:ssa_vs_ode}) plateau at these same values, Edge~3 holds as well, and
the three edges close the triangle, establishing mutual consistency of the
stochastic, deterministic, and stationary descriptions. The correct behaviour at the
$\koff=0$ and $\kdeg=0$ boundaries shows the agreement is not an artifact of the
particular parameter set.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have studied the telegraph model of stochastic gene expression from three
mathematically distinct viewpoints---exact stochastic simulation, closed moment
ODEs, and direct steady-state sampling---and shown that they agree. The central
contribution is not any single method but the \emph{triangular cross-validation}
that links them: the Law of Large Numbers ties the SSA to the moment ODEs in finite
time (Figure~\ref{fig:ssa_vs_ode}); the $t\to\infty$ fixed point of the ODEs
coincides with the analytical steady-state means; and the
Central-Limit-Theorem-rate convergence of the direct sampler
(Figure~\ref{fig:ss_convergence}), together with the long-time plateau of the SSA,
closes the loop. Each edge was confirmed numerically to within the expected
$1/\sqrt{N_{\mathrm{rep}}}$ statistical error, and the methods were further
validated on solvable edge cases.

Beyond verifying three correct implementations, this work illustrates a general
principle: when the same system can be described at several levels of abstraction,
those descriptions become mutual tests. A discrepancy along any edge would indicate
an error in one of the methods; their agreement supports our picture of
gene-expression noise as bursty, over-dispersed, but exactly characterised. Natural
extensions include adding a downstream protein species (the three-stage model),
allowing more than two promoter states, and using the validated SSA as ground truth
to benchmark approximate moment-closure schemes in regimes where exact closure is
unavailable.
