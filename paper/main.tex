\begin{abstract}
Gene expression is a stochastic process: even genetically identical cells in the same
environment produce very different numbers of messenger RNA (mRNA) molecules. The
telegraph model explains the main source of this variability. It treats a gene as a
two-state (ON/OFF) switch that transcribes mRNA only while active. We study the
telegraph model through three algebraically equivalent descriptions, all following from
the same chemical master equation, and check that their independent implementations
agree: we (i) generate stochastic trajectories with the Gillespie
stochastic simulation algorithm (SSA); (ii) derive and solve a closed system of
ordinary differential equations (ODEs) for the first and second moments; and
(iii) draw independent samples directly from the analytic steady-state
distribution, using the classic Beta--Poisson law together with an explicit
Bernoulli gene state. The main
contribution is the implementation cross-validation that links the three. The code is available at
\url{https://github.com/zakhar-guzii/telegraph-model-gene-expression}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Gene expression is variable.
Two cells with the same DNA, grown together in the same environment, can have different numbers of copies of the same mRNA or protein~\cite{elowitz2002,ozbudak2002}.
This difference between cells is called phenotypic noise. It happens naturally because of random molecular interactions. Experimentally this variability separates into two parts~\cite{elowitz2002}: \emph{extrinsic} noise, 
caused by differences between cells---their size, cell-cycle stage, or the pool of available polymerases---and \emph{intrinsic} noise, 
the randomness of the transcription and degradation events themselves, which persists even between two identical copies of a gene inside the same cell.

Inside a cell, the key players---a single copy of the gene, a handful of transcription factors,
and a few dozen mRNA molecules---are present in such small numbers that the discreteness and randomness of individual chemical events cannot be averaged away.
The telegraph model studied here is the simplest framework that describes this intrinsic component.
It treats the promoter as a two-state device that randomly switches between an active (ON) and an inactive (OFF) state~\cite{ko1991}.
While ON, it produces mRNA molecules through transcription in random bursts; each molecule is then degraded at a random time. As a result, the mRNA count is a discrete-valued stochastic process.

Despite its simplicity, the telegraph model reproduces the bursty, \emph{over-dispersed} mRNA distributions seen in single-molecule experiments, 
which count individual mRNAs inside single cells~\cite{raj2006}, and it can be analysed exactly~\cite{peccoud1995}. 
A convenient way to measure this spread is the \emph{Fano factor} $F=\sigma^2/\mu$~\cite{fano1947}, the ratio of the variance to the mean of the mRNA count. 
Pure Poisson production and decay, in which every event is independent, has variance equal to mean, giving the \emph{Poisson baseline} $F=1$. 
``Over-dispersed'' means $F>1$: the random switching of the gene adds extra variability on top of that baseline.

We treat the telegraph model as a case study in how a stochastic process can be described from several mathematically distinct viewpoints. 
We implement three approaches that look unrelated:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item a stochastic simulation (the SSA), which follows a single cell through time;
  \item a system of moment ODEs, which gives the population mean and spread
    deterministically;
  \item a steady-state sampler, which describes the population after a long time.
\end{enumerate}

Each is correct on its own, and the three are algebraically equivalent: all follow from the same
chemical master equation. What differs is the implementation. The three share no code and fail in
different ways---an error in the event loop, a mis-derived moment equation, or a wrong stationary law
would each disturb one description while leaving the others intact---so agreement between them is
evidence against implementation defects. It is not evidence about the master equation itself, which
all three inherit.

\section{Model Formulation}
\label{sec:model}

The state of the system at time $t$ is the pair
\begin{equation}
  \big(G(t),\,R(t)\big), \qquad G(t)\in\{0,1\}, \quad R(t)\in\mathbb{N},
\end{equation}
where the binary variable $G$ records whether the gene is OFF ($G=0$) or ON
($G=1$), and the integer $R$ counts the number of mRNA molecules present.
 Four basic reactions define the dynamics of the system. 
 Each reaction has a \emph{propensity} $a_j$, the rate at which it fires at any given moment:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{llll}
\toprule
\textbf{Reaction} & \textbf{Description} & \textbf{Effect} & \textbf{Propensity} \\
\midrule
$R_1$ & Gene activation   & $G:0\to1$       & $a_1 = \kon\,(1-G)$ \\
$R_2$ & Gene inactivation & $G:1\to0$       & $a_2 = \koff\,G$ \\
$R_3$ & RNA synthesis     & $R\to R+1$      & $a_3 = \ksyn\,G$ \\
$R_4$ & RNA degradation   & $R\to R-1$      & $a_4 = \kdeg\,R$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

All four rate constants are positive. The switching rates $\kon,\koff$ set how quickly
the promoter turns on and off, and their ratio fixes the long-run fraction of time the
gene is active; the factor $G$ in $a_3$ shuts transcription off entirely when the gene
is OFF; and $\kdeg$ is a per-molecule decay rate, so the total degradation propensity
$\kdeg R$ grows with the amount of mRNA present. Together the rates
$(\kon,\koff,\ksyn,\kdeg)$ and an initial condition $(g_0,r_0)$ at time $t_0$ fully
determine the law of the process.

\paragraph{Burst size.}
Once the gene is ON, the only
reaction that can switch it off fires at the constant rate $\koff$, so the duration of an active
period is exponential with mean $1/\koff$; during that period transcription fires at rate $\ksyn$.
The mean number of transcripts made per active period---the \emph{burst size} $B$---is therefore
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:burst}
  B = \ksyn\,\E[\text{ON duration}] = \frac{\ksyn}{\koff}.
\end{equation}
Bursts of this kind are observed directly in single cells~\cite{golding2005,raj2006}, and the exact
consequences of promoter switching for the mRNA distribution, including the limits recovered in
Section~\ref{sec:res-explore}, are derived in~\cite{iyerbiswas2009}.

Because each waiting time is exponential and depends only on the current state,
$(G(t),R(t))$ is a continuous-time Markov chain (CTMC). Its
distribution $P_t(g,r)=\Pr\{G(t)=g,\,R(t)=r\}$ evolves according to the chemical
master equation~\cite{vankampen,gardiner,wilkinson,ingalls}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:master}
\begin{aligned}
  \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t} P_t(g,r)
  ={}& \kon\big[\delta_{g,1}P_t(0,r) - \delta_{g,0}P_t(g,r)\big] \\
   &+ \koff\big[\delta_{g,0}P_t(1,r) - \delta_{g,1}P_t(g,r)\big] \\
   &+ \ksyn\,\delta_{g,1}\big[P_t(1,r-1) - P_t(1,r)\big] \\
   &+ \kdeg\big[(r+1)P_t(g,r+1) - r\,P_t(g,r)\big],
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
with the convention $P_t(g,-1)=0$.
Equation~\eqref{eq:master} is the basis for all three approaches below: the SSA samples trajectories whose distribution solves it, the moment ODEs are derived from it exactly,
and the steady-state sampler draws from its $t\to\infty$ fixed point.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Stochastic Simulation (Gillespie SSA)}
\label{sec:ssa}

The \emph{stochastic simulation algorithm} (SSA) of
Gillespie~\cite{gillespie1976,gillespie1977,gillespie2007} generates statistically
exact sample paths of a CTMC, making no approximation to the master
equation~\eqref{eq:master}. Its key idea is that, given the current state, two
questions separate: when does the next reaction occur, and which reaction is it?
With $a_0=\sum_{j=1}^{4} a_j$ the \emph{total propensity}, the waiting time is
$\tau\sim\mathrm{Exp}(a_0)$ and reaction $j$ fires with probability $a_j/a_0$.

Our implementation draws these by inversion, using two independent uniforms
$r_1,r_2\sim\mathrm{U}(0,1)$ per step from a seeded \texttt{numpy.random} generator~\cite{harris2020},
one seed per experiment:
\begin{equation}
  \tau = \frac{1}{a_0}\,\ln\!\frac{1}{r_1},
  \qquad
  j = \min\Big\{ k : \textstyle\sum_{i=1}^{k} a_i > r_2\,a_0 \Big\}.
\end{equation}
The time is then advanced by $\tau$, the state updated by reaction $j$, the reaction
propensities recomputed, and the loop repeats for a fixed number of events
$n_{\mathrm{sim}}$. If the total propensity reaches $a_0=0$, no reaction can
fire, so the state is absorbing and is held fixed afterwards.

A single trajectory is the history of one cell. To obtain population statistics, we run $N_{\mathrm{rep}}$ independent trajectories with identical parameters and
average over them. Each trajectory advances by its own random time increments, so the same event index occurs at different times in different trajectories.
We therefore resample every trajectory onto a shared uniform time grid by a zero-order hold before averaging;
the state is piecewise constant between events, so this introduces no error. The empirical moments are then ensemble averages at each grid time, for example
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ssa-mean}
  \widehat{\muR}(t)
  = \frac{1}{N_{\mathrm{rep}}}\sum_{i=1}^{N_{\mathrm{rep}}} R_i(t),
  \qquad
  \widehat{\muG}(t)
  = \frac{1}{N_{\mathrm{rep}}}\sum_{i=1}^{N_{\mathrm{rep}}} G_i(t),
\end{equation}
with sample variances and the sample gene--RNA covariance defined analogously.

\paragraph{Simulation horizon.}
The loop counter $n_{\mathrm{sim}}$ bounds the number of \emph{events}, not the elapsed time, so an
ensemble has no common end time. We therefore take the shared grid no further than the
earliest-finishing trajectory, $t_{\mathrm{end}}=\min_i t_i^{\,\text{last}}$: extending past that
point would freeze the exhausted trajectories at their final state and bias the ensemble toward
whatever those particular cells happened to be doing when they ran out of events. Each figure
therefore ends at its own, seed-dependent time, in every case a large multiple of the relaxation
time. This rule is not entirely neutral: a trajectory exhausts its event budget fastest when its total
propensity is high, so $t_{\mathrm{end}}$ is itself weakly correlated with the ensemble state, and we do
not attempt to quantify the resulting bias here.

Figure~\ref{fig:single-cell} shows a typical sample path generated by the algorithm. Averaging
many such trajectories according to~\eqref{eq:ssa-mean} gives the ensemble
moments that Section~\ref{sec:res-ssa-ode} compares with the moment ODEs of Section~\ref{sec:ode}.

\subsection{Moment ODEs}
\label{sec:ode}

The SSA describes individual cells. 
Often we instead want deterministic equations for population-level statistics---the \emph{moments}. 
A moment is an expectation: the mean $\muR=\E[R]$ is the average mRNA level, the variance
$\sigRsq=\E[R^2]-\E[R]^2$ measures the noise, and the covariance
$\CRG=\E[RG]-\E[R]\E[G]$ measures how gene activity and mRNA level vary together.

Applying the standard moment rule for a chemical master equation---the expected rate of
change of any observable is the sum over reactions of each propensity times the change it
makes---to $f=G,R,R^2,GR$ in turn yields four coupled equations for the telegraph
model~\cite{ingalls,wilkinson}:
\begin{align}
\label{eq:dmuG}
  \frac{\mathrm{d}\muG}{\mathrm{d}t} &= \kon\,(1-\muG) - \koff\,\muG , \\
\label{eq:dmuR}
  \frac{\mathrm{d}\muR}{\mathrm{d}t} &= \ksyn\,\muG - \kdeg\,\muR , \\
\label{eq:dsigR}
  \frac{\mathrm{d}\sigRsq}{\mathrm{d}t} &= 2\ksyn\,\CRG - 2\kdeg\,\sigRsq + \ksyn\,\muG + \kdeg\,\muR , \\
\label{eq:dCRG}
  \frac{\mathrm{d}\CRG}{\mathrm{d}t} &= \ksyn\,\sigGsq - (\kon+\koff+\kdeg)\,\CRG .
\end{align}
Each has a direct reading. Activation pushes mean activity toward $1$ and inactivation
pulls it toward $0$; mRNA is made in proportion to the active fraction $\muG$ and removed
in proportion to the amount present; the mRNA variance is driven up by the coupling to
gene activity (the $\CRG$ term) and by the randomness of production and decay, and down by
degradation; and the covariance is created by transcription passing the gene's own
fluctuations $\sigGsq$ on to the mRNA, then damped at the combined rate of switching and
degradation.

\paragraph{Exact closure.}
In a general nonlinear reaction system the equation for each moment depends on higher moments, so
the system never closes and one has to add approximate closure schemes~\cite{schnoerr2015,ingalls,wilkinson}.
Here it closes \emph{exactly}. The only possible nonlinearity is the variance of the gene state,
but because $G$ is binary,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:closure}
  \sigGsq = \E[G^2]-\muG^2 = \E[G]-\muG^2 = \muG\,(1-\muG),
\end{equation}
which depends on the mean $\muG$ alone. Putting~\eqref{eq:closure} into~\eqref{eq:dCRG} turns the
four equations \eqref{eq:dmuG}--\eqref{eq:dCRG} into a \emph{closed, exact} linear system in
$(\muG,\muR,\sigRsq,\CRG)$, whose solutions are the true moments of the master
equation~\eqref{eq:master}. A four-variable numerical integration is therefore enough, with no
closure approximation anywhere in the code.

\paragraph{Implementation.}
The four right-hand sides
\eqref{eq:dmuG}--\eqref{eq:dCRG}, with $\sigGsq$ substituted by~\eqref{eq:closure}, are coded
directly and passed to the explicit Runge--Kutta solver \texttt{scipy.integrate.solve\_ivp}~\cite{virtanen2020}
(method \texttt{RK45}, the embedded Dormand--Prince pair~\cite{dormand1980}) from the initial conditions $\muG(t_0)=g_0$, $\muR(t_0)=r_0$,
$\sigRsq(t_0)=\CRG(t_0)=0$, evaluated on a uniform grid of $1000$ time points. Section~\ref{sec:res-ssa-ode} overlays them on the SSA
ensemble at the same rates and the same initial condition.

\paragraph{Steady-state Fano factor.}
Setting the right-hand side of~\eqref{eq:dCRG} to zero gives
$\CRG^{ss}=\ksyn\,\sigGsq/(\kon+\koff+\kdeg)$ with $\sigGsq=\muG^{ss}(1-\muG^{ss})$
by~\eqref{eq:closure}; substituting that into~\eqref{eq:dsigR} at stationarity, where
$2\kdeg\,\sigRsq = 2\ksyn\,\CRG + \ksyn\,\muG + \kdeg\,\muR$ and $\ksyn\muG=\kdeg\muR$
by~\eqref{eq:dmuR}, and dividing by $\muR^{ss}$ yields the Fano factor in closed form:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:fano}
  F^{ss} \;=\; \frac{\sigRsq}{\muR}\bigg|_{ss}
  \;=\; 1 \;+\; \frac{\ksyn\,\koff}{(\kon+\koff)\,(\kon+\koff+\kdeg)} .
\end{equation}
The additive $1$ is the Poisson baseline: it is what
survives when $\koff\to0$ and the gene stops switching, so the mRNA count is a pure birth--death
process. The second term is the excess contributed by promoter switching, and it vanishes in two
distinct ways---as $\koff\to0$ (the gene is always ON) and as $\kon,\koff\to\infty$ at fixed ratio,
where a promoter that switches very fast averages out.

Setting the time derivatives in~\eqref{eq:dmuG}--\eqref{eq:dmuR} to zero in the same way gives the exact
stationary means used as the reference throughout Section~\ref{sec:results}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ss-means}
  \muG^{ss} = \frac{\kon}{\kon+\koff},
  \qquad
  \muR^{ss} = \frac{\ksyn}{\kdeg}\,\muG^{ss}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Steady-State Sampler}
\label{sec:ss}

As $t\to\infty$ the telegraph model forgets its initial condition and converges to a unique
stationary distribution $P_\infty(g,r)$. Instead of simulating a long transient to reach it, we can
sample from $P_\infty$ directly. Peccoud and Ycart~\cite{peccoud1995} showed that at steady state
the mRNA count follows the classic \emph{Beta--Poisson} law: it is Poisson, but with a mean that is
itself random from cell to cell. Let the \emph{mixing fraction} $p\in[0,1]$ be the long-run share of
time a cell's gene spends ON. Across a population $p$ is Beta-distributed, and given $p$ the mRNA
count is Poisson with mean $\lambda=c\,p$, where $c=\ksyn/\kdeg$ is the \emph{transcription
efficiency coefficient}: the mean mRNA level a permanently active gene would reach. With the
switching rates $a=\kon/\kdeg$ and $b=\koff/\kdeg$ (so $\kdeg$ fixes the unit of time),
the stationary mRNA law is therefore
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:beta-poisson}
  R \sim \mathrm{Poisson}(c\,p), \qquad p \sim \mathrm{Beta}(a,b).
\end{equation}

Averaging the Poisson kernel over the Beta density gives $P_\infty(r)$ in closed form, as a confluent
hypergeometric expression~\cite{peccoud1995}. We do not need it: because the
mixture~\eqref{eq:beta-poisson} \emph{is} that distribution, sampling the mixture samples $P_\infty$
exactly, at the cost of three vectorised draws per count and with no discretisation of the state space.

Our sampler draws the gene state as an explicit
$\mathrm{Bernoulli}(p)$ so we can also estimate the joint moments of $(G,R)$. For each of the
$N_{\mathrm{rep}}$ samples it draws
\begin{align}
  p &\sim \mathrm{Beta}(a,b),
  &&f(p) = \frac{\Gamma(a+b)}{\Gamma(a)\,\Gamma(b)}\,p^{\,a-1}(1-p)^{\,b-1}, \\[2pt]
  G \mid p &\sim \mathrm{Bernoulli}(p),
  &&\Pr(G=1\mid p)=p,\quad \Pr(G=0\mid p)=1-p, \\[2pt]
  R \mid p &\sim \mathrm{Poisson}(\lambda),
  &&\lambda = \frac{\ksyn}{\kdeg}\,p .
\end{align}
The key point is that the mRNA count $R$ is conditioned on the mixing fraction $p$, \emph{not} on
the current gene state $G$. Given $p$, the gene state and the mRNA count are drawn independently, so
a cell can have $G=0$ and still $R>0$---as in a real cell, where mRNA made during the last active
period survives past the switch to OFF.

\paragraph{The joint construction.}
Peccoud and Ycart~\cite{peccoud1995} derived the \emph{marginal} law of $R$, whereas the checks below need
$\widehat{\sigma}_G^{\,ss}$ and $\widehat{\CRG}^{\,ss}$, which are properties of the joint law. Coupling
$G$ to the same mixing fraction $p$ that drives the Poisson mean supplies one, and it reproduces the
covariance that the master equation gives independently. That reference value follows from
setting~\eqref{eq:dCRG} to zero, as in the Fano-factor derivation of Section~\ref{sec:ode}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:crg-cme}
  \CRG^{ss} = \frac{\ksyn\,\sigGsq}{\kon+\koff+\kdeg}
            = \frac{\ksyn\,\muG^{ss}(1-\muG^{ss})}{\kon+\koff+\kdeg} .
\end{equation}
Computing the same quantity from the sampler instead gives
$\E[RG]=\E\big[\E[R\mid p]\,\E[G\mid p]\big]=c\,\E[p^2]$. Since $\E[R]\E[G]=c\,\E[p]^2$, it follows that $\CRG = c\,\mathrm{Var}(p)$.
Using the Beta variance $\mathrm{Var}(p)=ab/\big((a+b)^2(a+b+1)\big)$ and substituting $ab/(a+b)^2=\muG^{ss}(1-\muG^{ss})$, $a+b+1=(\kon+\koff+\kdeg)/\kdeg$, and $c=\ksyn/\kdeg$,
this expands to
\begin{equation}
  \CRG = \frac{\ksyn}{\kdeg}\cdot\frac{\muG^{ss}(1-\muG^{ss})\,\kdeg}{\kon+\koff+\kdeg}
       = \frac{\ksyn\,\muG^{ss}(1-\muG^{ss})}{\kon+\koff+\kdeg},
\end{equation}
which matches~\eqref{eq:crg-cme} term for term. The construction therefore reproduces the joint moments
of the master equation. This is an algebraic identity rather than an empirical result: the numerical
agreement reported in Section~\ref{sec:res-ss} confirms the Monte Carlo estimator, not the construction
itself.

\paragraph{Boundary behaviour.}
The construction also behaves as expected at the edges of the parameter space.
As the switching rates become very slow compared with degradation ($a,b\to 0$), the $\mathrm{Beta}(a,b)$
density concentrates its mass at the endpoints $p=0$ and $p=1$, and the mixture separates into its building blocks:
a single Bernoulli trial for the gene state and, when ON, an ordinary Poisson count for the mRNA. At $\koff=0$
the gene never switches off ($p\equiv 1$) and the stationary law reduces to $\mathrm{Poisson}(\ksyn/\kdeg)$;
Section~\ref{sec:res-ss} tests this numerically. At $\kdeg=0$ no steady state exists and the sampler is
undefined.

The joint stationary law is the average of the conditional distributions over the mixing fraction,
$P_\infty(g,r) = \int_0^1 \Pr(G=g\mid p)\,\Pr(R=r\mid p)\,f(p)\,\mathrm{d}p$; drawing
$N_{\mathrm{rep}}$ triples $(p,G,R)$ and averaging gives unbiased Monte Carlo estimates of the
steady-state moments $\widehat{\muG}^{\,ss},\widehat{\muR}^{\,ss}$, and so on.

\section{Experiments and Results}
\label{sec:results}

Unless stated otherwise, the experiments use the symmetric \emph{reference parameters}
$\kon=0.5$, $\koff=0.5$, $\ksyn=20.0$, $\kdeg=1.0$, with $N_{\mathrm{rep}}=1000$ trajectories and initial condition
$(g_0,r_0)=(0,0)$, giving $\muG^{ss}=0.5$ and $\muR^{ss}=10$ by~\eqref{eq:ss-means}.
The parameter-exploration runs of Section~\ref{sec:res-explore} are the one exception: they
vary rates around a slower base of $\kon=\koff=0.1$, stated with each run in
Table~\ref{tab:param-exp}.
All three parts of the validation are implemented in Python; see the repository linked above.

\subsection{The Validation Triangle}
\label{sec:intersection}

The three descriptions of Section~\ref{sec:methods} must agree wherever their domains overlap. We state
those overlaps as the three edges of a \emph{validation triangle} and test each one numerically below.

\paragraph{Edge 1 --- SSA $\leftrightarrow$ Moment ODEs (finite time).}
The empirical averages~\eqref{eq:ssa-mean} are sample means of independent, identically distributed
trajectories, so by the Law of Large Numbers they converge as $N_{\mathrm{rep}}\to\infty$ to the true
expectations $\muR(t)$, $\muG(t)$ and $\CRG(t)$---which are exactly the solutions of the moment ODEs.
With finitely many replicates the SSA should therefore follow the ODE curves up to a statistical error
of order $1/\sqrt{N_{\mathrm{rep}}}$.

\paragraph{Edge 2 --- Moment ODEs $\leftrightarrow$ Steady-state sampler (long time).}
The stationary distribution is the $t\to\infty$ limit of the dynamics, so the moment ODEs must approach
a fixed point, which is~\eqref{eq:ss-means}. The steady-state sampler must estimate these same values,
with an error decaying as $\sigma/\sqrt{N_{\mathrm{rep}}}$ by the Central Limit Theorem, where $\sigma$
is the standard deviation of the stationary distribution. Observing that rate confirms the sampler is a
valid Monte Carlo estimator of the steady-state moments.

\paragraph{Edge 3 --- SSA $\leftrightarrow$ Steady-state sampler (closing the triangle).}
An SSA trajectory run long enough to reach stationarity samples the same distribution $P_\infty$ as the
direct sampler, so the long-time plateau of the SSA moments must agree with both the ODE fixed point and
the sampler estimates. Because both endpoints of this edge produce samples, the claim can be sharpened to
equality of the full laws, $\hat P^{\mathrm{SSA}}_\infty = \hat P^{\mathrm{smp}}_\infty$, rather than of
their first two moments alone; Section~\ref{sec:res-ss} tests it in total-variation distance.

\subsection{Parameter Exploration}
\label{sec:res-explore}

This part runs the SSA on its own to check that it reproduces the expected behaviour when the
gene's parameters change. We vary one mechanism at a time so that each run isolates a single effect
(Table~\ref{tab:param-exp}). The final run changes numerical settings rather than
biological mechanisms, testing the stability of the simulation itself.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.49\textwidth]{param_switching_slow.png}
  \hfill
  \includegraphics[width=0.49\textwidth]{param_switching_fast.png}
  \caption{Switching speed reshapes noise at a fixed mean ($\muR\approx10$). Slow switching (left,
    $\kon=\koff=0.05$) produces long bursts, a wide $\pm\sigma_R$ band, and strong
    gene--RNA covariance. Fast switching (right, $\kon=\koff=10$) averages the
    promoter out, leaving a narrow band and near-zero covariance.}
  \label{fig:wf-switch}
\end{figure}

Three results emerge from these runs.
First, switching speed sets the noise at a fixed mean: as the switching rates grow relative to
$\kdeg$ (Figure~\ref{fig:wf-switch}), the gene state averages out, $\sigRsq$ and $\CRG$ fall, and $F$
drops toward the Poisson baseline without reaching it at any finite rate---the behaviour predicted by the
exact limits of~\cite{iyerbiswas2009}.

Second, with $\kon$ fixed, the burst size~\eqref{eq:burst} sets the steady-state mean $\muR^{ss}$. The
expression runs show that a weaker promoter makes fewer molecules but has a larger relative spread
(coefficient of variation, $\sigma_R/\muR$), which is why genes expressed at low copy number appear noisier.

Third, the steady state depends on neither the initial condition nor the numerical resolution. The two
initialisations reach the same Fano factor, as they must for an ergodic chain, and enlarging the ensemble
reduces sampling error without moving the steady state. The smaller ensemble is set to the cell counts
typical of single-molecule mRNA studies~\cite{raj2006}.

\subsection{SSA vs.\ ODE: Moment Dynamics (Edge 1)}
\label{sec:res-ssa-ode}

Edge~1 of Section~\ref{sec:intersection} asserts that the stochastic simulation and the deterministic
moment ODEs describe the same dynamics at all times. The direct test is the overlay in
Figure~\ref{fig:ssa_vs_ode}.
The SSA ensemble averages follow the deterministic ODE curves throughout the transient relaxation and into
the steady state.
The visible deviations are confined to the covariance curve, the noisiest of the three statistics, and shrink
as $N_{\mathrm{rep}}$ increases.
The $\pm\sigma$ bands are wide, but they measure cell-to-cell variability rather than numerical error, and
they match the analytical spread predicted by the moment ODEs.

The agreement is not limited to the symmetric case: the SSA tracks the deterministic dynamics under slow and
fast switching, high and low expression, and both initial conditions. Figure~\ref{fig:wf-ssaode} shows the
least favourable of these, the high-noise slow-switching regime, where the tracking is unchanged.

\subsection{Steady-State Validation (Edges 2 \& 3)}
\label{sec:res-ss}

Edges~2 and~3 of Section~\ref{sec:intersection} concern the stationary regime. This section tests both,
and checks that all three descriptions reduce correctly to the known limits at the boundaries of
parameter space.

The ODE solutions in Figure~\ref{fig:ssa_vs_ode} plateau at the analytical fixed points~\eqref{eq:ss-means},
and the direct sampler reproduces the same values to within Monte Carlo error.
Figure~\ref{fig:ss_convergence} makes the convergence quantitative: on log--log axes the mean absolute
deviation of the sampler's $\muR^{ss}$ estimate follows a $-1/2$ slope over four decades.

The boundary limits predicted in Section~\ref{sec:ss} also hold. At $\koff=0$ the model reduces to a
birth--death process with a Poisson steady state~\cite{vankampen,gardiner}: the moment ODEs relax to
$\muG^{ss}=1$ and $\muR^{ss}=\ksyn/\kdeg$, and the sampled counts overlay the analytic Poisson PMF
(Figure~\ref{fig:wf-poisson}) with a measured Fano factor of $0.99 \pm [\text{INSERT\_SE}]$ against the
theoretical $1$. At the opposite boundary, $\kdeg=0$, mRNA accumulates without bound and no steady state
exists.

\paragraph{Edge 3 as a distributional test.}
Two different distributions can share a mean, a variance, and a covariance, so agreement of the first two
moments is weaker evidence than it appears. Since both endpoints of Edge~3 produce samples, we can compare
the full stationary laws instead, using the Total Variation distance
$\mathrm{TV}=\tfrac12\sum_r|\hat P^{\mathrm{SSA}}(r)-\hat P^{\mathrm{smp}}(r)|$. Unlike a $\chi^2$ test, TV
needs no binning of near-empty states, is bounded in $[0,1]$, and is the largest probability by which the two
laws can differ on any event. On the SSA side the mRNA count is recorded at one late time point per
trajectory, which costs samples but avoids the autocorrelation that would understate the spread.

The null distribution for TV is obtained by \emph{direct resimulation}: assuming both methods sample the same
stationary law, we draw 200 fresh pairs at the same sample sizes and recompute the statistic. We avoid
bootstrapping each side from its own empirical measure, which would add sampling noise twice and centre the
null band above the true expected TV, making the test too lenient.

The slow-switching regime (Figure~\ref{fig:edge3}, right) is the demanding case, and the one where
moment-matching is least informative. Its stationary law is bimodal, with mass at $R=0$ (long OFF periods)
and near $R=\ksyn/\kdeg$ (long ON periods) and the mean falling in the trough between them. A method could
match $\muR$ and $\sigma_R$ exactly and still miss this shape entirely; the $\pm\sigma$ bands used elsewhere
in this paper suggest a Gaussian profile that this law does not have.

Measured by TV, the two laws agree. In the slow regime $\mathrm{TV}=0.0117$ against a null median of $0.0130$
(95th percentile $0.0166$, one-sided $p=0.74$); in the symmetric regime $\mathrm{TV}=0.0093$ against a median
of $0.0155$ (95th percentile $0.0191$, $p=1.00$). Neither exceeds what sampling variation alone would produce.

The symmetric case falls below all 200 null draws, hence $p=1.00$. This value indicates that our
resimulated null distribution may be conservative, or that the empirical variance matched the analytic
expectation closely enough to flatter the test statistic. The second reading is consistent with what we
observe: the empirical variance of $59.8$ matches the analytic $\sigRsq=60$ to within $0.4\%$, and
splitting the sample in half reproduces the dispersion of two independent sampler draws. Calibrating the
null across many seeds would separate the two explanations; we did not do so here. Under either reading,
Edge~3 compares the hand-written SSA loop against a sampler that follows in closed form
from~\eqref{eq:beta-poisson}.

stationary distribution rather than its first two moments.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

All three edges of the validation triangle hold. Their value lies in what the three implementations do
not share: a trajectory simulator, a deterministic ODE integrator, and a Beta--Poisson sampler have no
common code and fail in different ways, so an error in the SSA event loop, a mis-derived moment equation,
or a wrong stationary law would each disturb one edge while leaving the others intact. The reductions to
classical limits at $\koff=0$ and $\kdeg=0$ anchor this at the two boundaries where the answer is known
in advance.

The triangle is a validation of the three implementations against one another over the regimes tested,
not a proof of correctness over the whole parameter space; and because all three descend from the same
master equation~\eqref{eq:master}, it cannot detect an error in that shared starting point. What it
provides is a template: any extension of the model can be held to the same three-way standard, and the
next section describes two such extensions.

\section{Future Work}
\label{sec:future}
With the telegraph model established as a validated baseline, two extensions would capture more of the underlying biology. A third direction would strengthen the validation itself: a direct numerical solution of the master equation by finite-state projection would supply an external reference for the full time-dependent law, extending the triangle beyond mutual consistency between our own implementations.

\paragraph{Incorporating the protein level.}
The natural next step is a three-stage model (gene $\to$ mRNA $\to$ protein) that includes translation at rate $k_{\mathrm{trans}}$ and protein degradation at rate $k_{\mathrm{deg},p}$. 
The tools validated here carry over directly: the SSA handles the two additional reactions unchanged, and the moment ODE system expands to cover protein variance and mRNA--protein covariance.

\paragraph{Gene regulatory networks.}
In vivo, gene products often toggle other genes, forming regulatory networks that can be described by mapping the interacting genes to an \emph{Ising model}, where ON/OFF states act as spins and regulatory links as couplings.
The obstacle is the size of the state space: for $n$ genes the joint distribution spans $2^n$ promoter configurations, each multiplying the unbounded mRNA and protein spaces, so direct integration gives way to approximate high-dimensional density estimators.
